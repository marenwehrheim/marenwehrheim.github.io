Differences In Dynamic Reconfiguration Of Whole-Brain Connectivity Are Related To Individual Differences In Working Memory Task Performance

Maren Wehrheim1,2, Christian Fiebach1,3,
1Goethe University, Frankfurt 2Frankfurt Institute for Advanced Studies 3Brain Imaging Center, Frankfurt
Paper Supplementary Code arXiv -->

Abstract

The ability to store information in working memory critically depends upon whole-brain network states and their dynamic adaption to specific cognitive demands. Here we use data from the Human Connectome Project (N=331) to study how whole-brain connectivity dynamics differ (i) between task and non-task (rest), (ii) between low (0-back) and high (2-back) task demands, and (iii) how task-related connectivity differences relate to between-person differences in working memory performance. Dynamic whole-brain connectivity was quantified as cofluctuation time series, i.e., the instantaneous co-activation strength between brain regions. Time-averaged cofluctuation during task is similar to that during rest, however two-back co-activation is lower than zero-back co-activation (both p<.001). We suggest that this reflects suppression of predominant but task-unrelated networks with increasing cognitive task demands. Focusing on connectivity dynamics, we observed that depending on the preprocessing strategy the strength of whole-brain cofluctuation jumps to a higher level or slowly decreases towards the end of each task block. Fixation intervals between blocks exhibit significantly different levels of cofluctuation than during task. Lastly, we find that task performance is positively correlated to zero-back cofluctuation strength (r=.18, p<.001) but negatively correlated to cofluctation strength and dynamic variability during the 2-back task (Strength: r=-.26, standard deviation during a block: r=-.22, rateof change during a block: r=-.24, p<.001). This may indicate that better performing subjects deviate less from resting-state under easy task conditions, but can adapt their whole-brain connectivity patterns more strongly under increased task demands. Overall, we show that differences in reconfiguration of whole-brain connectivity states are related to cognitive task performance.
